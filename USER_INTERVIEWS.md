# User Interviews

## Interview 1 — Senior Network Security Engineer

**Role / Team:** Senior Network Security Engineer, working independently.

**Context:**  
A highly technical individual working solo, with very little paid AI tooling in use.

### Key Quotes
- “It’s expensive.”
- “A shareable link.”
- “Keep it internal.”

### What stood out
This interview made it clear that not every technical user is deeply invested in paid AI tools yet. For this user, the main pain was not tool overload, but the feeling that AI spend can still become expensive without obvious proof of value.

### What it changed in the product
This reinforced the need for StackTrim to be honest when savings are low. Instead of forcing recommendations, the product should clearly say when a stack is already fairly efficient and still provide a useful shareable result for internal discussion.

## Interview 2 — Student Developer / Hackathon Team Contributor

**Role / Team:** Student developer, usually working in 3–6 person project teams during hackathons and college projects.

**Context:**  
A small team environment where tool choices are often based on affordability and ease of use.

### Key Quotes
- “Sometimes people stop using a tool after the initial excitement.”
- “API usage feels the hardest to judge because costs can suddenly increase depending on usage.”
- “I would trust a savings recommendation if it clearly showed actual usage data, comparisons, and explained why a cheaper option makes sense.”

### What stood out
This interview was especially useful because trust was more important than price alone. The strongest concern was whether a recommendation could actually be explained and justified, not whether it sounded clever.

### What it changed in the product
This directly supported the decision to keep the audit engine deterministic and explainable. It also reinforced the need for transparent formulas, confidence levels, and recommendation reasoning instead of vague AI-generated suggestions.

## Interview 3 — Co-founder, Deep-Tech Startup Media Department

**Role / Team:** Co-founder handling media and web-development work in a small deep-tech startup team of 4–5 people.

**Context:**  
A startup-style team where the discussion was less about raw subscription cost and more about practical workflow value.

### Key Quotes
- “API usage.”
- “Everything.”
- “Less token limit and more hallucination.”

### What stood out
This interview showed that reliability matters as much as cost. The concern was not just how much tools cost, but whether they were dependable, usable, and accurate enough to avoid workflow friction.

### What it changed in the product
This reinforced the decision to keep AI summaries secondary to deterministic logic. It also pushed the product toward a more honest, usage-fit oriented audit rather than simply recommending cheaper alternatives in a generic way.

## Interview 4 — UI/UX Designer and Prototype Testing Team

**Role / Team:**  
UI/UX designer and prototype tester working in a 5-member product/design team.

**Context:**  
The interviewee works across UI/UX design, prototype testing, and lightweight data analysis workflows. Tool decisions are mainly handled directly by them.

### Key Quotes
- “API usage.”
- “Proper example showing the evidence.”
- “Share it with a co-founder.”

### What stood out
This interview reinforced how important explainability is for trust. The interviewee repeatedly emphasized the need for proper evidence and reasoning behind recommendations rather than generic optimization claims.

### What it changed in the product
This directly reinforced the decision to make recommendation reasoning transparent and easy to understand. It also validated the addition of detailed breakdowns, confidence indicators, and shareable reports that can be reviewed internally with cofounders or team members before making subscription decisions.

### Additional Insights
The interviewee also mentioned that many AI products fail to properly understand user intent or workflow context before generating recommendations. This reinforced the importance of keeping StackTrim focused on practical workflow-fit reasoning instead of relying heavily on AI-generated assumptions.

## Interview 5 — Full-Stack Engineer

**Role / Team:**  
Full-stack engineer with expertise in optimizing SaaS, subscription, and cloud spending.

**Context:**  
Deep experience with SaaS cost optimization, stack efficiency, and startup economic modeling.

### Key Quotes
- “I don’t want to optimize too aggressively.”
- “A small improvement could still be valuable.”
- “Share it with my co-founder.”

### What stood out
This interview reinforced that even small savings matter if they come with clear reasoning. The interviewee emphasized that overly aggressive or complex optimization advice can be counterproductive, validating StackTrim’s conservative and explainable approach.

### What it changed in the product
This reinforced the decision to keep recommendations realistic and conservative. It also validated the importance of shareable audit reports for internal discussion, especially among cofounders or technical leads who need to align on stack decisions.

## Patterns Across Interviews

### 1. Trust matters more than hype
All three interviews in different ways pointed toward skepticism of generic or inflated recommendations. A report must explain itself clearly and avoid sounding like marketing.

### 2. API usage is harder to judge than seat-based subscriptions
This came up repeatedly. Seat plans are easier to understand, while API spend feels less predictable and more variable.

### 3. Shareability is valuable
A clean shareable link was seen as useful, especially for internal discussion or cofounder review.

### 4. Internal discussion is often the next step
For teams that find savings, the most likely action is not immediate purchase or cancellation, but internal conversation first.

### 5. Productivity matters alongside price
A good recommendation cannot only say “cheaper.” It also has to explain whether a tool still helps the team work effectively.

---

## Product Decisions Influenced by These Interviews

- Keep savings recommendations conservative and explainable.
- Do not manufacture savings for already-efficient stacks.
- Make public share pages clean, useful, and safe to send internally.
- Treat API spend as a special case because it is harder to estimate.
- Emphasize workflow impact, not just raw price differences.
- Keep AI limited to summary text, not financial reasoning.

---

## Final takeaway

These interviews made StackTrim feel like a trust-first product rather than a flashy AI feature. The strongest recurring theme was that users want clear reasoning, realistic savings, and a result they can actually share with a team or cofounder.
